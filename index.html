Beyond the star rating

Urban planning has traditionally leaned on static numbers — population density, land-use zoning, footfall counts — indicators that are usually years behind how a city is actually lived and felt. Meanwhile, every review left on a crowdsourced platform is a small, geotagged confession of how someone actually experienced a place. Stitched together at scale, thousands of those confessions start to behave like a dataset, not just anecdote. That's the premise this project runs on: treat consumer sentiment as spatial data, not just a marketing metric.

Ambient geospatial data

Digital geographers have a name for this: Ambient Geospatial Data — precise coordinates fused with subjective, text-based opinion, produced passively by ordinary people going about their lives rather than by a formal survey. Kuala Lumpur's F&B scene is an unusually rich source of it. Social-media momentum, viral exposure and deliberately "Instagrammable" interiors turn trendy restaurants into constant subjects of commentary — which makes them a near-ideal laboratory for testing whether satisfaction spreads through a neighbourhood or stays locked to a single venue.

"Must-try" isn't a neutral phrase in KL — it's closer to a five-star endorsement. Most sentiment engines have no idea.

Why KL needed its own lexicon

Most off-the-shelf sentiment models carry an implicit Western socio-linguistic bias — trained on English-language corpora that reflect North American or European norms. Point that at Kuala Lumpur's hybrid, code-switching review culture and it stumbles: colloquial Malay, Hokkien-inflected phrases, and the aspirational jargon of local food influencers all get flattened into generic, often wrong polarity scores. Power spicy reads as enthusiasm here, not a warning label. Worth it (berbaloi) carries more emotional weight than its literal English translation. Ignoring that isn't a small rounding error — it distorts the emotional signal entirely. That's the gap SentiLexM, the bilingual lexicon at the centre of this pipeline, was built to close.

Who this is for

Three groups get something out of this, in different ways. Spatial data scientists get a working example of unstructured, crowdsourced text being folded into a GIS workflow the same way structured survey data usually is. F&B operators and investors get a specific, testable claim: proximity to a "hot" commercial strip does not, on this evidence, buy you a sentiment premium — so capital may be better spent on product and service than on postcode. Planners and small-business owners without access to expensive desktop GIS software get a free, browser-based dashboard that puts the same spatial statistics in front of them, no licence required.

What was in scope — and what wasn't

The dataset is deliberately bounded: 75 food and beverage establishments across the Kuala Lumpur federal territory, chosen because they were culturally labelled "trendy," "viral," or "aesthetic" rather than through a citywide census. The corpus behind it is 7,336 scraped reviews, narrowed down to 5,915 validated entries after cleaning. It's strictly textual — no photos, no reviewer metadata, no internal business financials or footfall counts. That's a study of public sentiment, not of business performance, and the findings should be read that way.

Full methodology, formulas and code excerpts are covered in the next section.

The Problem

Star ratings flatten everything. Location tells you why.

A 4.5-star badge can't tell you whether the love for a place is genuine, spread across the city, or squeezed into one hyped-up postcode. And most sentiment tools weren't built for how Kuala Lumpur actually talks.

No spatial context

Every review is an island

A single rating evaluates one venue in isolation. It can't reveal whether satisfaction is business-specific, or shaped by outside forces — tourist-zone pricing, transit access, or a neighbourhood-wide reputation effect.

Language bias

Generic sentiment engines don't speak KL

Tools trained on Western English corpora miss the valence of berbaloi (worth it), power spicy, or bising (too noisy). The result: flattened, inaccurate polarity scores for culturally rich feedback.

Method — three phases

From messy text to a coordinate on a map.

Built entirely in Python. Each review moves through the same three-stage pipeline before it ever touches the map.

01

Acquisition & geocoding

7,336 raw Google Maps reviews scraped across 75 curated "trendy" F&B establishments in Kuala Lumpur. Every venue is geocoded via Nominatim into precise WGS 84 (EPSG:4326) latitude/longitude pairs.
02

Cleaning & SentiLexM scoring

Text is lowercased and stripped of links, punctuation, emojis and digits via RegEx, then tokenized and matched against SentiLexM — a bilingual lexicon built for Malaysian colloquialisms and F&B slang (sedap, mahal, berbaloi). Raw weights (−5 to +5) are rescaled to a 1.0–5.0 polarity index.
S_final = (S_raw + 5) × 0.4 + 1
03

KDE hotspot mapping

Kernel Density Estimation transforms 75 discrete, sentiment-weighted points into a continuous raster surface — rendered live via Python's folium.plugins.HeatMap inside an interactive WebGIS dashboard.

The map lies (a little)

High sentiment isn't locked into KLCC — it's spread across the whole city.

Eyeball a plain map and it looks like every great venue sits on the KLCC–Bukit Bintang axis. That's where the densest red cluster forms. But run real spatial statistics instead of trusting the human eye — which is wired to see clusters even where none exist — and a different picture emerges: exceptional dining experiences are relatively evenly spread across the broader urban landscape, not exclusively isolated to one commercial hub.

Why it matters

Location premiums don't buy better sentiment.

If proximity to a "hot" commercial strip doesn't guarantee higher scores, the old real-estate logic needs a rethink — at least for F&B.

For operators

Invest in the plate, not the postcode

Venues outside the primary commercial corridors face no statistical sentiment penalty. Product differentiation, service training and quality control move the needle more than an address ever will.

For planners

Democratised spatial intelligence

Packaged into an open-source WebGIS dashboard, this analysis lets planners, analysts and small-business owners explore normalised sentiment maps and venue-level metadata — no proprietary desktop GIS required.

What's next

Four ways to make this sharper.

01

Live data streams

Swap static scrapes for a real-time feed (e.g. Google Places API) to turn this into an always-on sentiment monitor.

02

A smarter lexicon

Iteratively expand SentiLexM to auto-detect emerging urban slang, emojis and regional dialects as they evolve.

03

Multivariate modelling

Overlay sentiment against transit accessibility, demographics and menu pricing to explain why hotspots form.

04

Geographic scaling

Extend beyond KL's federal territory into the wider Klang Valley to test whether decentralised excellence holds.
